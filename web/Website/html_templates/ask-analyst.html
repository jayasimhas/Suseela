Indian Vaccine Makers Seek Funding 'Quantum Leap' To Stay Competitive

  • 27 Nov 2016
  • Analysis
  • Penelope MacRae

SharedContent.ExecutiveSummary

Indian vaccine makers are pushing for their government to increase what they call “sub-optimal” funding to help the industry fend off rising competition from Chinese and other rivals, and to help a potential transition to the more challenging development of biosimilars.

Competition globally and locally is heating up for Indian vaccine companies, especially as they seek to develop therapeutic vaccines for cancer and products for use in adults alongside long-standing preventive and pediatric jabs.

The vaccine producers are calling for a “quantum leap” in soft government funding for product development to address new medical challenges and fend off foreign rivals which are aggressively developing in the field.

“India’s vaccines industry has attained a position of leadership globally and we need to maintain that,” Dr. Rajesh Jain, joint managing director of Indian firm Panacea Biotec Ltd., told a recent industry conference in New Delhi organized by the Confederation of Indian Industry (CII).

China Threat

India’s half-dozen main domestic vaccine players and smaller emerging firms are eyeing with particular alarm giant neighbor China, which has the largest vaccine-making capacity in the world and has been promoting the manufacture of such products as an export growth industry.

China’s economies of scale could mean tough pricing competition both in India and in other countries. Indian firms say they fear the country, which has already grabbed much of India’s active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) market with costs 50-60% lower than local prices, may pose a similar threat with its vaccines.

V.G. Somani, joint drugs controller at India’s Central Drugs Standard Control Organization (CDSCO), told the New Delhi conference that the Indian government is aware of the challenges facing the industry and noted, in particular, that the country faces a potential “big bomb in the form of China” with its “huge manufacturing capacity.”

Home Grown Challenges

On their home turf, Indian companies say problems include insufficient government financial support, an obstructive regulatory maze that delays bringing vaccines to market, and competition from global pharmaceutical companies which are selling vaccines in India as they bolster their local footprint.

“While opportunities are knocking so are challenges, and Indian companies are increasingly facing competitors from other countries which have benefited from scale-up funding,” Jain observed. “The dearth of such options in India impacts the competitiveness of the Indian industry,” he said, adding, “We need a quantum leap in the level of soft funding” for product development.

The vaccine makers called the government’s decrease in tax benefits on research and development spending “very discouraging especially in a segment like vaccines where the industry is aspiring to build global competitiveness based on technology strength.’’

The last budget cut previous tax deductions of 200% for in-house R&D expenditure to 150% for 2017-20, and then down to 100% in the years following. This is in sharp contrast to more generous allowances elsewhere for R&D, said a report prepared on behalf of the vaccine makers by the CII, a leading business group, which was presented to the Nov. 10 conference.

Asian Competitors

Some other Asian countries are generous in their support. In Singapore for instance, a base level 100% deduction is given for R&D topped by an additional 50%. On top of that concession, the city state grants a 250-300% “enhanced” deduction, the report noted.

In Malaysia, a 200% “super deduction” is given along with an investment tax allowance of 100% on capital R&D expenses.

South Korea, too, offers tax incentives and goes to the extent of financing equipment and providing leased land for facilities. The South Korean government’s investment in biosimilars is at least 35% of total R&D costs, says business group FICCI.

Against this background, Jain urged the introduction of “globally comparable fiscal incentives.” But India’s top drug regulatory official, G.N. Singh, told the conference that government funding is tight. “We don’t have deep pockets,” he said.

CDSCO’s Somani urged vaccine makers to team up on innovation. “Please hold hands and take the risk -- it has to be shared. It cannot be only the government.” Also, he said, while the government was committed to removing regulatory bottlenecks, the industry was sometimes a “divided house” and needed to speak about its requirements with one voice.

Funding Pressure

The Indian vaccine industry began as a network of state-owned manufacturers supplying basic childhood vaccines to the national immunization program that were later joined by private firms. Its main business has been developing cheaper generic versions of vaccines already available in the West.

Industry sales posted a robust CAGR of 25% between 2011 and 2015 and notched $1bn in sales last year. But firms complain profit margins are thin with the government, their main customer, constantly seeking to drive down prices.

“The risks are taken by the companies, but when it comes to procurement, you [the government] want dirt cheap prices. It’s not on,” Panacea’s Jain told the meeting.

The problem of funding is becoming more acute as India moves from “a generic mindset to an innovative phase” as it seeks to replicate in the biosimilar field the success it has enjoyed in generic production, added Jain. “The vaccine industry needs to continuously engage in product development to remain competitive.”

But developing biosimilars is far more complex and costly and there are still gaps in India’s skill base. And already India is behind in vaccine trials, Singh noted. India has 136 trials vaccine trials underway while China has more than double that number at 285.

“The Indian vaccine industry should be supported by appropriately large national funding programs,” Jain urged.

Immunization Programs

Also vaccine makers say the government must hike spending on immunization to bolster the industry. Ironically, while India is a top producer and exporter of affordable vaccines, it lags way behind global peers in immunizing children. (The Indian government’s total healthcare spending represents just 1% of GDP, one of the lowest rates in the world.)

India’s full national immunization coverage stands at 65%, far below Mexico’s 88%, Brazil’s 90%, Vietnam and Turkey’s 93% and China’s 99%, World Health Organization figures show. India’s child immunization rates are also below its South Asian neighbors and among the lowest outside sub-Saharan Africa.

Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s administration has introduced a new campaign, Mission Indradhanush, with the aim of achieving 90% vaccination coverage by 2020 against seven vaccine-preventable diseases: diphtheria, whooping cough, tetanus, polio, tuberculosis, measles and hepatitis B. This could represent the biggest vaccine access initiative in immunization history.

But vaccine manufacturers say it’s still not clear from where the money for the program will come from as “the current spend is sub-optimal.” A country-wide immunization campaign in 2014 was boosted by $500m from international vaccination funding alliance GAVI. But by 2021, India’s growing GDP will mean it no longer qualifies for GAVI’s backing and must become self-financing.

China already is no longer eligible and GAVI says it expects India to “completely transition” out of its support in five years.

Export Challenges

Also, the Indian vaccine industry says it feels vulnerable on the export front. India currently exports vaccines to 150 countries, accounts for 60% of GAVI’s vaccine volume supplies, and produces up to 90% of the demand for some WHO vaccines.

The WHO in 2011 said China had met “international standards for vaccine regulation.” The decision opened the doors for Chinese vaccines to be submitted for WHO prequalification approval so they can be bought by UN agencies and GAVI. As a result, a number of Chinese vaccine producers are now ramping up vaccine R&D and expanding production capacity.

Indian vaccine producers’ appeal for more funding chimes with an earlier warning by Pharmexil, an agency under India's commerce ministry whose mandate is to promote the drugs industry. “Unless the government comes forward to help Indian vaccine makers with capacity building by offering soft funds, adequate power supply and infrastructure, Indian vaccine makers will not be able to meet the Chinese challenge.”
